# Jared Spool (Global) — Full Learnings

## 1. Designing for Pleasure

**Problem:** Product usable but not pleasurable. Users tolerate, not enjoy.
**Mainstream:** Optimize for efficiency and ease.
**Their approach:** Design for pleasure: delight, surprise, beauty. Functional excellence plus emotional reward.
**Example:** Checkout process that's easy AND delightful through thoughtful copy, encouraging feedback.

## 2. UX Research as Continuous Practice

**Problem:** Research done once, at beginning. Findings become stale.
**Mainstream:** Research phase, then design, then build.
**Their approach:** Research continuous throughout design. Weekly user testing reveals real issues. Design evolves.
**Example:** Every sprint: user testing. Findings directly shape next sprint work.

## 3. The Seductive Details Debate

**Problem:** Small delightful details distract from core task.
**Mainstream:** Focus on core functionality. Delight is secondary.
**Their approach:** Seductive details increase pleasure if core task is excellent. Without core excellence, details are distracting.
**Example:** Micro-animation is delightful if checkout is frictionless. If checkout is broken, animation is annoying.

## 4. User Expectations as Design Constraint

**Problem:** Design innovates. Breaks user expectations. Confusing.
**Mainstream:** Innovation over convention.
**Their approach:** User expectations are real constraints. Break them consciously, for good reason. Respect conventions.
**Example:** Hamburger menu: convention now. Breaking it needs justification, not just novelty.

## 5. The Design of Everyday Things Principles

**Problem:** Product logic doesn't match physical world logic.
**Mainstream:** Design is intuitively obvious.
**Their approach:** Make mental model explicit through signifiers and affordances. User mental model should match system model.
**Example:** Slider affordance (knob appearance) signals dragging. Knob shape affords interaction.

## 6. Knowability and Discoverability

**Problem:** Feature exists but user can't find it.
**Mainstream:** Users should explore and find features.
**Their approach:** Features should be discoverable: through exploration or signaling. Balance hiding (simple) and showing (discoverable).
**Example:** Swipe gesture not obvious; show hint first time. User discovers, learns.

## 7. User Testing with Participants

**Problem:** Design reviewed by team. Real users react differently.
**Mainstream:** Internal review. Designer decides.
**Their approach:** User testing with actual participants reveals assumptions. Participants interact differently than designers expect.
**Example:** Designer thinks feature is obvious. User testing shows it's hidden. Redesign based on finding.

## 8. The Power of Iteration

**Problem:** Perfect design from beginning. Unlikely.
**Mainstream:** Get design right first. Minimize iteration.
**Their approach:** Multiple iterations improve design. First version never best. Iterate, test, improve.
**Example:** Design iterated 5 times before shipping. Each iteration better based on testing.

## 9. Context-Specific Design

**Problem:** Design assumes stable context. Real usage is varied.
**Mainstream:** Design for primary use case.
**Their approach:** Design for multiple contexts: different places, different times, different emotional states, different needs.
**Example:** Mobile app used in noisy environment, at night, by one-handed user. Design for all.

## 10. Measuring User Experience

**Problem:** UX feels subjective. Can't measure quality.
**Mainstream:** Measure efficiency (task time, error rate).
**Their approach:** UX measurable through research: satisfaction, task completion, emotional response, willingness to recommend.
**Example:** Survey: 'Would you recommend this to friend?' Answer predicts retention and reviews.

## 11. The Role of Emotion in UX

**Problem:** Emotion seems subjective. Focus on functionality.
**Mainstream:** Feelings come from task completion.
**Their approach:** Emotion is core UX. How user feels shapes how they perceive usability. Delight matters.
**Example:** User frustrated by product perceives it as harder than it is. Happy user perceives as easier.

## 12. Accessibility Expands Audience

**Problem:** Accessibility reduces audience. Wrong.
**Mainstream:** Accessibility is niche concern.
**Their approach:** Accessibility benefits everyone. Captions help loud environment. Large type helps eyes. Accessibility expands, doesn't limit.
**Example:** Video captions help when sound is off. Benefit: everyone, not just deaf users.

## 13. The Myth of Intuitive Design

**Problem:** Design should be intuitive. But intuition comes from learning.
**Mainstream:** Intuitive design requires no learning.
**Their approach:** Intuition is learned familiarity. Design that matches existing patterns feels intuitive. Novel design requires learning.
**Example:** iPhone gesture control felt intuitive because touch was already familiar. It required learning.

## 14. Error Prevention Over Error Recovery

**Problem:** Design allows errors, then provides recovery.
**Mainstream:** Perfect error messages help.
**Their approach:** Better to prevent error: constraints, confirmations, clear language. Error prevention > error recovery.
**Example:** Form prevents invalid entry (only numbers in number field). Better than accepting invalid then correcting.

## 15. Content Strategy as UX

**Problem:** Content separate from design. Copy is leftover.
**Mainstream:** Design UX. Writers provide copy.
**Their approach:** Content is central to UX. What system says, how it says it, shapes experience. Content strategy is UX strategy.
**Example:** Button label 'Learn More' vs. 'Start Free Trial': different experience, same button.

## 16. The Danger of Hubris

**Problem:** Designers confident about solutions. Wrong.
**Mainstream:** Trust your design instinct.
**Their approach:** Assume you're wrong. Test to find out how. Overconfidence leads to missed problems.
**Example:** Designer sure navigation is clear. Testing shows 40% of users confused. Assumption was wrong.

## 17. User Research Methods

**Problem:** What research method reveals truth?
**Mainstream:** Quantitative data is objective.
**Their approach:** Different methods reveal different truths: interviews reveal why, surveys reveal prevalence, observation reveals behavior.
**Example:** Quantitative: 60% don't use feature. Qualitative: users don't understand feature exists.

## 18. Design Language as Communication

**Problem:** Interface inconsistent. Users confused by variety.
**Mainstream:** Variety keeps design interesting.
**Their approach:** Consistent design language: button styles, icons, patterns, color, type. Consistency is clarity.
**Example:** All buttons look same: shape, color, interaction. Users learn pattern. Fluency increases.

## 19. The Innovation Paradox

**Problem:** Innovation requires novelty. But novelty confuses.
**Mainstream:** Innovate in every aspect.
**Their approach:** Innovate in core functionality. Keep interaction pattern familiar. Novelty in features, not in how to use.
**Example:** Email innovation (Gmail): new organization method. But email interaction (type, send) familiar.

## 20. User Goals vs. Business Goals

**Problem:** Business goals conflict with user goals. Design caught between.
**Mainstream:** Balance both.
**Their approach:** Alignment possible: design serves both. User goal of easy communication aligns with business goal of engagement.
**Example:** User wants quick checkout. Business wants conversion. Faster checkout = more conversions. Aligned.

## 21. Engagement Without Addiction

**Problem:** Engagement can be manipulative. Creates unhealthy habits.
**Mainstream:** Maximize engagement. Use dark patterns.
**Their approach:** Ethical engagement: valuable to user, respects autonomy, not dependent on manipulation.
**Example:** Push notification: genuinely useful to user, not just attention-grabbing.

## 22. The Importance of Prototyping

**Problem:** Design on paper. Implementation misses intention.
**Mainstream:** Document design. Build from documentation.
**Their approach:** Prototype early, often. Prototype reveals what static design can't. Interactive prototypes are best specifications.
**Example:** Static wireframe misses interaction complexity. Interactive prototype reveals issues early.

## 23. Scaling Design Thinking

**Problem:** Design works for small projects. Scales to large organization?
**Mainstream:** Design process is context-dependent.
**Their approach:** Design principles scale: user research, iteration, testing. Organizational structures may differ.
**Example:** Startup: one designer. Enterprise: 100 designers. Both use research, iteration, testing.

## 24. The Influence of Typography on UX

**Problem:** Type is formatting. Doesn't affect interaction.
**Mainstream:** Type choice for aesthetics.
**Their approach:** Type deeply affects readability, comprehension, emotional response. Type choice shapes UX.
**Example:** Serif font feels formal. Sans-serif feels modern. Type choice sets tone, affects UX.

## 25. Metrics That Matter

**Problem:** Measuring wrong things. Vanity metrics mislead.
**Mainstream:** Track usage, engagement, clicks.
**Their approach:** Measure what matters: task completion, user satisfaction, retention, actual goals.
**Example:** High clicks on feature that doesn't convert is vanity metric. Focus on conversion.

## 26. The Kano Model: Three Forces Governing Satisfaction

**Problem:** Teams don't know when adding a feature will delight users vs. when fixing a bug matters more.
**Mainstream:** Add features users ask for; treat bugs as separate from UX investment.
**Their approach:** The Kano Model reveals three distinct forces: Basic Expectations (must work or users are frustrated, but working perfectly does not impress), Performance Payoff (more investment generates proportionally more delight), and Excitement Generators (unexpected features that produce disproportionate delight). Features migrate from Excitement Generator to Performance Payoff to Basic Expectation as they propagate through the market. Designers need to monitor all three simultaneously.
**Example:** Flickr's personalized homepage was an Excitement Generator when it launched — users described it as "exciting." As every site adopted dashboards, it became a Basic Expectation. Today, a site without it would frustrate users even though its presence no longer delights them.

## 27. The UX Tipping Point

**Problem:** Organizations say design matters but keep shipping mediocre experiences.
**Mainstream:** Hire designers, create a UX team, and hope the culture changes.
**Their approach:** The "UX Tipping Point" is the moment an organization stops compromising on design. The journey follows recognizable stages: UX Dark Ages → Spot UX Projects → Serious UX Investment → Embedding UX Into Teams → Integrated UX and Services. Startups with design-from-day-one founders skip the journey. Organizations that never cross the tipping point lose to those that do.
**Example:** Disney crossed the tipping point with the $1B Magic Band investment. A decade earlier, guests had to call customer support just to complete a reservation online. The Magic Band refused to cut any corner — they wired an entirely new infrastructure into the parks rather than compromise the experience.

## 28. Design Is the Rendering of Intent

**Problem:** "Design" is treated as a function performed only by people with the title of designer.
**Mainstream:** Design is what designers produce in design tools; everyone else executes that output.
**Their approach:** Design is the rendering of intent. When any person makes a choice about how something should be, they are designing. Every developer, product manager, legal reviewer, and general manager who influences a product's outcome is making design decisions — whether they know it or not. The design team is everyone who influences the rendering of intent.
**Example:** Netflix performance engineers set bandwidth thresholds that determine when the buffering spinner appears. That spinner moment is a user experience decision even though no one on that team holds a design title. As Spool argues, they become UX designers the moment a viewer's video stops.

## 29. Five Design Decision Styles

**Problem:** Teams either apply full user research to every project (expensive, slow) or skip research entirely (produces poor results).
**Mainstream:** Either research everything or trust your gut.
**Their approach:** Five styles exist on a spectrum of research investment: (1) Unintended Design — no design consideration at all; (2) Self Design — designers build for themselves; (3) Genius Design — draw on accumulated past research experience; (4) Activity-Focused Design — research specific user tasks and workflows; (5) User-Focused Design — research goals, contexts, motivations, and the full experience. The most effective teams are skilled in all five and choose based on the project's novelty and stakes.
**Example:** An REI photographer used Genius Design (years of watching customers examine boot treads in stores) to photograph boots with the tread showing — and this unknown "quirk" turned out to be the reason REI outsold L.L. Bean on identical products. A first-time e-commerce team can't rely on Genius Design they don't have; they need User-Focused research.

## 30. Exposure Hours: The Single Biggest Predictor of Great UX

**Problem:** Teams have dedicated UX researchers who report findings, yet still produce confusing products.
**Mainstream:** Hire researchers; distribute research reports; assume knowledge transfers.
**Their approach:** Research reports don't transfer the impact of direct observation. There is a direct correlation between the number of hours each individual team member is personally exposed to real users and the quality of that team member's design decisions. This is not delegation — every person who influences the product must have their own direct exposure. The minimum effective dose is two hours every six weeks per person.
**Example:** The U.S. Digital Service's Dana Chisnell had 130 developers and product managers making design decisions on a citizenship application system. None of them had ever met a user. Once she got developers into field offices to watch field officers process applications, their design decisions transformed. The people who went first came back making choices their unexposed colleagues couldn't explain — which motivated the rest to go.

## 31. Designing in the Gaps Between Activities

**Problem:** Products deliver a list of discrete features but create a fragmented, frustrating overall experience.
**Mainstream:** Identify user tasks; design screens for each task; deliver the list of features.
**Their approach:** Great experiences are designed in the gaps between activities — not just during the activities. Six Flags designs discrete rides; Disney designs the full day: the Character Breakfast that opens the day, the seamless paths between rides, the fireworks finale, and the origami towels waiting at the hotel room. The gaps are where the experience lives.
**Example:** UberCab's real innovation wasn't the ride itself; it was what happened between activities — the single-press request, the GPS tracking while waiting, the digital receipt sent immediately after, and the mutual ratings system. Every gap was filled with thoughtful design. Users pay a premium fare because the gaps are better, not because the ride is better.

## 32. Hunkering: Deliberate Visual Disorientation

**Problem:** Designers become too familiar with their own work to see where it diverges from their original intention.
**Mainstream:** Internal review, design critique sessions, and documentation reviews.
**Their approach:** "Hunkering" is the craftsperson's practice of laying out work-in-progress in roughly its final form, stepping back, and experiencing visual disorientation — the gap between what they imagined and what they've actually built. This disorientation forces a moment of reconciliation: either the work needs correction or the mental model of the goal needs adjustment. Master craftspeople do this frequently, sometimes multiple times per day.
**Example:** A master cabinetmaker studied by Spool's team would lean raw materials against the wall in their approximate final positions, step back with a "quizzical expression," and stare. He called this "hunkering." When digital designers send early prototypes through usability tests, they are hunkering — letting the reality of the design confront their mental image of it.

## 33. Market Maturity Determines Design Strategy

**Problem:** Designers push for better UX but organizations resist; designers blame management for not valuing design.
**Mainstream:** The organization just doesn't understand the value of design.
**Their approach:** Market maturity — not management attitude — determines whether UX investment pays off. Stage 1 (Technology): a new product with no competitors; users tolerate any design because they have no choice. Stage 2 (Features): competitors emerge; customers compare feature checklists; design still can't be measured easily. Stage 3 (Experience): features are parity; design becomes the competitive differentiator. Stage 4 (Commodity): design and price are indistinguishable. Design strategy must match the stage.
**Example:** A ferry ticketing website (Stage 1 — the only way to book that ferry) has no financial incentive to improve its confusing interface. Its customers have no alternative. The same investment would be essential for an e-commerce retailer in a crowded market where customers can switch to a competitor with a better checkout experience in seconds.

## 34. The Design-Infused Organization

**Problem:** Having designers embedded in every team still leaves the majority of product decisions made without design thinking.
**Mainstream:** Hire more designers; put one in every team; count design headcount.
**Their approach:** A design-infused organization is one where every team member — not just designated designers — makes design a primary consideration. This requires three steps: (1) regular and frequent exposure to users for every team member (minimum: 2 hours every 6 weeks); (2) a shared aspirational experience vision that everyone can describe the same way; and (3) a culture of continuous learning from experiments and mistakes. Without all three, organizations may embed designers but still not be design-infused.
**Example:** One organization's development team changed their design decisions after watching users interact with the product for two hours. A developer described changing an interface because they watched a user become frustrated the week before. That's design-infused behavior — a developer making design-quality decisions without being asked.

## 35. Experience Vision as Organizational Compass

**Problem:** Teams all claim to be working toward a better experience, but each member describes the goal differently.
**Mainstream:** Write a vision statement; put it in a presentation deck.
**Their approach:** An experience vision is "a flag on a stake on the horizon" — everyone can see it and marches toward it. The test: ask each team member to write down the key milestones of their experience vision. If their descriptions differ, the vision is not real. A real vision can be told as a story, and everyone on the team tells it the same way. Spool's test: everyone can tell Hansel and Gretel identically; if they can't tell your product vision identically, you don't have one yet.
**Example:** A company crafted a journey map of the current user experience showing frustrating lows. They then asked: "What if we eliminated every frustrating moment?" They wrote that story from the user's perspective. That became their experience vision. Every subsequent design decision was judged by one question: does this get us closer to or further from the flag?

## 36. A/B Testing Without Research Creates an Experimentation Cargo Cult

**Problem:** Teams use A/B testing as a proxy for design understanding.
**Mainstream:** Run A/B tests; implement winning variants; repeat.
**Their approach:** A/B testing without prior qualitative research is "random monkeys testing random designs against random criteria." It tells you whether someone converted, not whether that's what they wanted or why they did it. Heavy investment in A/B testing produces design debt, prevents genuine UX innovation, and turns teams into "blind followers" of results they can't explain — a cargo cult approach where no one knows why anything works.
**Example:** When challenged about Amazon's A/B testing culture, Spool posed this question: name a major innovative UX capability that Amazon e-commerce has introduced in the last five years. The experimentation culture optimizes existing patterns but works against creating new ones. Amazon stays competitive through logistics and network effects, not through UX innovation.

## 37. Storytelling Is the Core of Design Communication

**Problem:** Designers can't keep teams aligned when design decisions need to be made by many people.
**Mainstream:** Write detailed design specifications; present wireframes; defend decisions in meetings.
**Their approach:** The medium of design is behavior, and storytelling is the best tool for communicating desired behavior across a team. Scenarios — stories of users' lives that put design in motivational context — are more powerful than specifications because everyone can internalize them. When the designer shifts to the role of narrator, the team members craft the design to fit the story rather than execute the designer's instructions.
**Example:** A photo-sharing site running a User-Focused research scenario discovered that users frequently browse photos from strangers they admire. This finding wouldn't appear in activity-focused testing. The story of "someone browsing a photographer's work because they admire their style" opened up new feature ideas — a following system, discovery feeds — that would never have emerged from "users upload and share photos."

## 38. Scenarios Give User Stories Their Full Context

**Problem:** User stories in development backlogs capture the task but not the user's surrounding context, leading to incomplete implementations.
**Mainstream:** Write user stories in "As a user, I want to, so that" format; execute each independently.
**Their approach:** Scenarios give user stories their motivational context — what brought the user to this moment and what they need to do after. When developers understand both the user story and the scenario, they can identify critical adjacent functionality the story alone would miss. Without the scenario, teams build technically correct but contextually wrong solutions.
**Example:** A password reset user story ("As a customer, I want to reset my password so I can log in") doesn't tell the team that this user was responding to a marketing campaign and, after reset, should be returned to the campaign landing page — not dumped on the generic homepage. The scenario contains the detail that changes the implementation.

## 39. Design Leadership as Competency Building in Non-Designers

**Problem:** Design leaders focus on producing better artifacts rather than raising the design capability of the whole team.
**Mainstream:** Protect design decisions from non-designer interference; design for the team, not with them.
**Their approach:** A design leader's primary job is to help every team member become consciously competent at design — able to recognize the difference between good and bad design, even if not yet able to create good design independently. The four stages of competence (Unconscious Incompetence → Conscious Incompetence → Conscious Competence → Unconscious Competence) require different interventions. Design systems support fluency. Field exposure builds literacy.
**Example:** Dana Chisnell at the U.S. Digital Service had a team of 130 non-designers making the bulk of design decisions for a citizenship application system. Her job was not to design for them but to make them consciously competent. Getting developers to field offices — where they watched users process applications — moved them from unconscious incompetence to conscious incompetence: they could now recognize bad design when they saw it.

## 40. Expectancy Bias Shapes What Design Teams Produce

**Problem:** Design leaders hold low expectations of non-designers' ability to contribute to design quality.
**Mainstream:** Non-designers can't be trusted to make design-quality decisions; protect design from their interference.
**Their approach:** The Rosenthal-Fode experiments demonstrated that the expectations held about performers change their actual performance — "smart rat" and "dull rat" labels produced objectively different results from identical animals. The same effect operates in design teams: treating non-designers as capable of design literacy produces better outcomes than treating them as design threats. Set high expectations; they will perform to match them.
**Example:** Teachers told that blue-eyed students were academically superior to brown-eyed students produced measurably better test results from blue-eyed students by year's end — with no other intervention. In design teams, designers who treat developers and product managers as capable design thinkers (and build systems to support them) produce more coherent products than designers who treat them as obstacles.

## 41. Experience Strategists Connect User Pain to Executive Priority

**Problem:** No one in the organization bridges the gap between what users struggle with and what executives care about.
**Mainstream:** UX teams present research findings; hope executives act on them.
**Their approach:** An experience strategist works at the executive level, identifying the overlap between deep user pain and the organization's top strategic priorities, then orchestrating designers, engineers, data analysts, and marketing to deliver solutions. They don't wait for a mandate. They just start doing the work. The key skill is translating user research findings into the business outcomes executives care about: revenue, cost reduction, retention.
**Example:** Daniella, an experience strategist at a software company, learned that small business owners couldn't figure out how to use the Pay Now feature. She also learned that executives desperately needed a new revenue stream. She connected these two facts, convinced designers to build a prototype of an improved feature, worked with data analytics to project revenue impact, got executive backing, coordinated with marketing on the launch, and delivered a tenfold increase in Pay Now revenue — all without a formal mandate.

## 42. Journey Maps Gain Power When Aspirational Overlays the Current

**Problem:** Journey maps document what is frustrating but don't show where to go.
**Mainstream:** Map the current experience; present frustration points to stakeholders; prioritize fixes.
**Their approach:** Journey maps reach their full potential when the aspirational future experience is overlaid on the current experience. The space between the current journey and the aspirational journey becomes a visible design vision. Teams can track progress by plotting where the current design sits between the two states. Every design decision becomes measurable: does this move us toward or away from the aspirational journey?
**Example:** A product team created a journey map showing the current invoicing experience — frustration peaks when waiting weeks to get paid. Their aspirational journey showed payments arriving within three days. That gap on the map focused the entire organization's priorities: what can we build to move from the current journey to the aspirational one?

## 43. Captive Audiences Don't Reward UX Investment

**Problem:** Designers invest in improving UX for products whose users have no alternatives, and the investment gets no traction.
**Mainstream:** Good UX is always the right investment, regardless of market context.
**Their approach:** In Stage 1 markets (no competition, captive audience), users tolerate any design because they have no choice. UX investment rarely generates a financial return in this context. Enterprise software, intranets, and regulated monopolies often occupy Stage 1. The financial case for UX investment requires that customers have real choices — i.e., Stage 2 and beyond.
**Example:** A ferry reservation website that is the only way to book a crossing has remained frustrating for years. Many users require multiple attempts; some can't complete a booking without calling. Yet the company has no financial incentive to improve it: the ferry runs regardless of the website's quality, and customers have no alternative booking method. The same amount of UX investment in a competitive e-commerce environment would produce measurable revenue results.

## 44. The Genius Design Trap

**Problem:** Experienced designers skip research because they "already know" the answer from prior work.
**Mainstream:** Experience is a substitute for research; seasoned designers can rely on their accumulated knowledge.
**Their approach:** Genius Design only works when past experience was built on actual user research — not just design experience. "Just having designed the functionality before doesn't count, unless the research also went with it." Designers who built products without thorough research carry false confidence, not genuine expertise. Calling it Genius Design when it's really Self Design leads to confidently wrong decisions.
**Example:** A designer who built five e-commerce shopping carts — each accompanied by thorough user research and follow-up evaluation — can design a sixth without the same level of research and get it right. A designer who built five carts entirely from their own judgment, without research, has accumulated five rounds of unverified assumptions, not genuine expertise.

## 45. Design Literacy, Fluency, and Mastery Are Three Distinct Levels

**Problem:** Organizations send teams to "design workshops" expecting a single intervention to solve all design-quality problems.
**Mainstream:** Train teams once; assume competency follows.
**Their approach:** Three distinct levels require three different types of intervention. Literacy (recognizing good from bad design) comes from direct exposure to users — watching people use the product. Fluency (executing consistently with the right process) comes from practice with tools like design systems and frameworks. Mastery (adapting to novel, complex situations spontaneously) comes from extensive experience combining research and execution over time.
**Example:** A product manager who can recognize a frustrating checkout flow when they see it (literacy) still needs process support to avoid creating one themselves (fluency). Giving them a design system they understand keeps them on the rails. Neither literacy nor fluency produces the spontaneous judgment of an expert who can design a sixth shopping cart from experience — that requires mastery.

## 46. "Make New Mistakes": A Culture of Continuous Learning

**Problem:** Organizations treat failure as career risk; teams play safe, learn nothing, and repeat the same mistakes.
**Mainstream:** Minimize failure; control for risk; celebrate only what worked.
**Their approach:** The U.S. Digital Service's unofficial motto — "Make New Mistakes" — captures the right approach: failure is expected in complex design work, but repeating the same mistake signals that learning didn't occur. The goal is learning, not infallibility. Teams need safe sandboxes for experiments, retrospectives to analyze what happened, and cultures where reporting what failed is as celebrated as reporting what succeeded.
**Example:** An organization that ships a feature that frustrates users and then systematically studies why — adjusting their next design accordingly — produces better long-term outcomes than an organization that never ships anything controversial. The second organization hasn't avoided bad decisions; it's just deferred them.

## 47. Fringe Behaviors Reveal the Biggest Innovation Opportunities

**Problem:** Teams research the obvious primary use cases and miss adjacent behaviors that could define the product's next generation.
**Mainstream:** Research the core use cases; design for the mainstream user flow.
**Their approach:** User-Focused Design research looks beyond what users are doing to what they're also doing — fringe behaviors that fall outside the designed intent. These "fringe ideas" are hard to discover through activity-focused research but can produce delightful functionality that transforms user engagement. The team doing User-Focused research consistently finds innovation that teams doing Activity-Focused research miss entirely.
**Example:** Research on a photo-sharing site revealed that users frequently browse photos from strangers they don't know — because they admire their photographic skills or subject choices. This is pure fringe behavior (the site was designed for sharing your own photos). Discovering it opened up features for following photographers, exploring by style or subject, and discovering new photographers — which became the basis for what eventually made photo platforms social rather than just personal.

## 48. Proactive Research: Build User Knowledge Before Questions Arise

**Problem:** UX research is reactive — triggered by a product question that needs answering or a feature being evaluated.
**Mainstream:** Commission research to answer specific product questions; conduct studies when needed.
**Their approach:** The most impactful UX strategy comes from building deep user knowledge proactively — before specific product questions arise. When a team already understands their users' lives deeply (their pressures, workflows, frustrations, and aspirations), they can identify opportunities that no stakeholder thought to ask about. This knowledge is the raw material of strategy, not just of individual design decisions.
**Example:** Daniella spent months "hanging out" with small business owners before any specific product question was posed. She watched them go about their business, learned the details of their daily lives, and built knowledge no one else in her company had. That knowledge — acquired before anyone asked her to answer a specific question — is what let her identify the Pay Now opportunity and connect it to executive priorities.

## 49. Design Systems Enable Non-Designer Fluency

**Problem:** Non-designers making product decisions without design training create inconsistent, incoherent products.
**Mainstream:** Audit and correct non-designer decisions after the fact; have designers review everything.
**Their approach:** Design systems and pattern libraries are fluency tools — they allow non-designers to make design-consistent decisions without needing design mastery. When the whole team is fluent in the design system (understanding what each component is, how it works, and why it exists that way), they produce coherent designs faster. This is the technical infrastructure that makes a design-infused organization operationally feasible.
**Example:** A developer who understands the design system can choose the right component for a new screen — button vs. link vs. toggle — without asking a designer for approval. If they understand why each element exists as it does, their choice will be consistent with the product's visual and interaction language. Without that fluency, they'll improvise and create inconsistency.

## 50. Context Is Where Design Happens

**Problem:** Designers evaluate designs on aesthetic and usability merit alone, disconnected from the organizational and technical context in which they must be implemented.
**Mainstream:** Judge designs by how well they serve users; present the best design to stakeholders.
**Their approach:** Design is always embedded in context: organizational constraints, technical infrastructure, business model, stakeholder politics, and market stage. A beautiful design that ignores these constraints is not a better design for the organization — it's an impossible design. Great design requires understanding both the intention (what you want to achieve for users) and the context (what it will take to render that intention in the real world).
**Example:** Tyler Thompson's boarding pass redesign was visually far superior to Delta's. But implementing it would require replacing more than 10,000 thermal printers worldwide with 4-color printers on white stock with bleed edges, creating an entirely new ink supply chain, and re-engineering every airport printer system. The context makes the "better" design genuinely worse for Delta. Spool argues designers who ignore context may be producing good-looking artifacts rather than good design.
