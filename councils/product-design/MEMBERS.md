## Saptarshi Prakash (Indian) — Flipkart / Indian Product Design

**Key principle:** Design for the India that exists, not the India you imagine.
**When to invoke:** Indian user behaviour, low-bandwidth UX, mobile-first product decisions, onboarding for first-time digital users, vernacular product design.

- The Indian user's mental model is shaped by physical-world experiences — design with that, not against it
- Low bandwidth is not an edge case in India; it is the primary condition
- Vernacular language in UI is not a localisation feature; it is an inclusion decision
- First-time smartphone users and power users coexist in your user base — design the seam between them
- Trust signals in Indian product design are different: density of information often reads as credibility, not clutter
- Onboarding must assume nothing; literacy, language, and device familiarity cannot be taken for granted
- Community and social proof carry outsized weight in Indian user decisions
- Delight in Indian product design is often found in micro-interactions that acknowledge the user's effort
- Voice and video are not premium features; they are primary interaction modes for large segments
- The best Indian product design is invisible to the user but deeply researched by the designer

## Luke Wroblewski (Global) — Google / Mobile-First Pioneer

**Key principle:** Mobile first is not a screen-size decision — it is a priority decision.
**When to invoke:** Mobile UX, form design, input patterns, progressive disclosure, touch interface decisions.
*Note: Extrapolates from global (primarily US) mobile design. Flag when applying to Indian 2G/WebView context.*

- Design for the smallest screen first; adding for larger screens is easier than subtracting
- Forms are the single highest-friction point in most products — every field needs a justification
- Input design determines whether users complete or abandon; this is not a detail
- Progressive disclosure: show only what is needed at this moment, reveal complexity on demand
- Touch targets have physics — 44px minimum is not a guideline, it is anatomy
- One-handed use is the default; design for the thumb's natural arc
- Inline validation is always better than post-submission error messages
- Mobile data entry is painful; reduce it wherever possible without sacrificing data quality
- The fold does not exist on mobile in the same way — scrolling is natural, but the first screen must earn it
- Speed is a feature; perceived performance matters as much as actual performance

## Jared Spool (Global) — UIE / Design Research

**Key principle:** Good design is invisible. Bad design creates friction the user blames themselves for.
**When to invoke:** Usability research, design decision frameworks, user mental models, experience quality measurement, design team structure.
*Note: Extrapolates from US enterprise and consumer product design. Flag when applying to Indian school teacher context.*

- The user's experience is the sum of all their interactions, not just the designed ones
- Usability problems are always design problems, never user problems
- The gap between the user's mental model and the system's model is where all friction lives
- Research without synthesis is data collection; synthesis is where the value is
- Design at the edges (error states, empty states, edge cases) reveals the quality of design thinking
- Accessibility is not a feature for a minority; it improves the experience for everyone
- Design decisions made without observation are guesses, not design
- The best way to improve design quality is to increase the team's exposure to actual users
- Writing is design; the quality of UX copy is inseparable from the quality of UX
- Metrics that don't connect to user outcomes are vanity metrics

## Steve Krug (Global) — Usability / Don't Make Me Think

**Key principle:** Users don't read — they scan. Design for scanning, not reading.
**When to invoke:** Information hierarchy, navigation, cognitive load, web/app usability, user testing protocol.
*Note: Extrapolates from US web usability (2000s–2010s era). Flag when applying to mobile-first Indian product context.*

- The most important question in usability: does the user know what to do next?
- Scanning is the default reading mode online; design for it rather than fighting it
- Conventions exist for a reason — the cost of breaking them must exceed the benefit
- Navigation must answer three questions instantly: where am I, what's here, where can I go?
- Home page is not the front door anymore; any page can be the entry point
- Happy path design is easy; designing for confusion and error is the real skill
- Eliminating one source of confusion is worth more than adding one new feature
- User testing with five users finds most usability problems; you don't need large samples
- Visual noise is anything the eye must process and decide is irrelevant — reduce it
- The best error message is the one that never has to appear

## Andrew Chen (Global) — a16z / Network Effects

**Key principle:** The Cold Start Problem is the first and hardest product design challenge for any networked product.
**When to invoke:** Network effects, two-sided marketplace design, cold start strategy, viral growth, retention architecture, product-market fit for social/marketplace products.
*Note: Extrapolates from US tech (Uber, Airbnb, Slack, Tinder, LinkedIn case studies). Flag when applying to Indian market dynamics.*

- Every networked product must find its atomic network first — the smallest group that self-sustains without outside intervention
- Two-sided networks have a "hard side" — the harder-to-acquire participant whose presence makes the product valuable for everyone else; design and economics must solve for them first
- The magic moment is when a new user first experiences the core value of the network; the entire onboarding should be engineered to reach this moment as fast as possible
- "Come for the tool, stay for the network" — a single-player utility can bootstrap network adoption before the network itself has value (Instagram's filters, LinkedIn's resume)
- Networks don't tip automatically — they require deliberate strategies (invite-only, geographic concentration, manual simulation) to cross the threshold from fragile to self-sustaining
- The engagement effect, acquisition effect, and economic effect are the three compounding forces that create escape velocity; identify which one is driving growth and invest there
- All acquisition channels decay over time (Law of Shitty Clickthroughs) — the only sustainable advantage is a product people talk about because of the network value itself
- Retention is the foundation; a leaky bucket (80%+ day-30 churn is normal for consumer apps) cannot be fixed with acquisition spend — fix retention before scaling growth
