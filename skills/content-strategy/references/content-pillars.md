# Content Pillars

These 5 pillars are data-derived — each is validated by actual YouTube
completion rates, Instagram saves/shares, Dovetail user research, or
platform review data. They are not hypothetical frameworks.

---

## Pillar 1: Career & Salary

**What it covers:** Salary ranges by school type (IB, IGCSE, CBSE, State board,
Govt), school tier, city and subject. Career paths, progression timelines,
job switching patterns, offer negotiation, how to evaluate a school offer.

**Why it works:** Highest YouTube views and completion rates in the channel.
IB Teacher salary: 4,134 views, **74.49% completion**. IGCSE: 3,146 views,
**73.54% completion**. Teachers watch 10+ minute salary videos in full because
this data does not credibly exist anywhere else. The information is high-value
and scarce.

Meta Ads confirm this: the salary-intent ad set converted at **CPR ₹98** vs
₹162 for review-intent content — 40% cheaper per landing page visit. Salary
content drives high-intent clicks both organically (YouTube) and paid (Meta).

**Audience fit:** All 3 segments, but strongest for Segments 2 and 3 (private
school teachers, career-mobile). Segment 1 (govt) cares about pay scales and
in-hand salary vs. nominal, not school choice.

**Format principles:**
- Specific numbers only. "₹22,000/month" not "low salary." "IB Math PGT in
  Bengaluru: ₹45,000–65,000" not "international school teachers earn more."
- Named school types and cities. Bengaluru first (44% of traffic).
- Credibility anchor: "Based on X experiences shared on staffroom" when
  using platform data.
- Long-form YouTube: strong fit. Short-form: use as a teaser with a
  specific data hook, point to long-form.

**Language note:** Meta Ads data shows Kannada-English (KaEn) content converts
at CPR ₹144 vs ₹279 for English-only in the Bengaluru-focused campaign — nearly
2x better. For salary content targeting Bengaluru, bilingual (Kannada/Hinglish +
English) delivery significantly outperforms English-only.

**Topic ideas:**
- Salary reality check: CBSE private vs. IGCSE vs. Government (with numbers)
- What do IB schools actually pay? (specific breakdown)
- "I switched from govt to private — here's the real salary difference"
- How to evaluate a school offer before saying yes
- Why do teachers in Bengaluru earn more than in [city]?

## Pillar 2: Teaching Practice

**What it covers:** Classroom techniques that work in Indian school contexts,
especially low-resource settings. AI tools for teachers. Lesson plan approaches.
Handling disengaged students. Subject-specific pedagogy.

**Why it works:** AI Tools video: 3,417 views, **69.73% completion** — strong
signal for practical tool content. Dovetail confirms: all 3 segments want
practical techniques, not abstract pedagogy. Segment 1 specifically wants
low-cost, low-resource classroom ideas. Segment 2 wants AI tools and worksheets.
Segment 3 wants innovative approaches that go beyond the standard lesson plan.

**Audience fit:** Strongest for Segments 1 and 2. Segment 3 is skeptical of
generic CPD ("you learn more from classroom presence than CPD") — for them,
content must be specific, teacher-sourced, and grounded in real classroom
moments.

**Format principles:**
- "Here's what worked and why" — not generic tips
- Indian classroom context is mandatory: student demographics, resource
  constraints, curriculum pressure
- Teacher-to-teacher voice: "This is what a teacher tried, and it worked"
- Segment 1 filter: would this work with ₹0 budget and no projector?

**Topic ideas:**
- AI tools every Indian teacher can use today (free, practical, no jargon)
- Teaching Chemistry/Math/English to kids who don't want to learn
- "I taught particles + tolerance — here's how a science concept became
  a life lesson" (Sugandhi quote as content template)
- How to plan a lesson for mixed-ability classrooms
- What actually works in low-income private school classrooms

## Pillar 3: School Workplace Reality

**What it covers:** What it is actually like to work at different types of
schools. Management quality, workload, job security, facilities, how
decisions get made. The information teachers need before joining a school
but can never find.

**Why it works:** "Why Do Teachers Change Schools Frequently" — most
mission-aligned content, strong share rate. This pillar is staffroom's
structural product thesis: teachers leave schools because of workplace
reality, and no platform helps them research it before joining. Platform
data (635+ reviews) is the unique source for this pillar.

**Audience fit:** All segments, but the insight need is sharpest for
Segment 2 (private low income — higher school-switching rates, fewer
protections) and Segment 3 (experiencing "client relationship" school
culture and disillusioned by it).

**Format principles:**
- Never name a specific school negatively — staffroom does not accuse
- Data-backed framing: "X% of teachers on staffroom say management
  doesn't allow feedback to the principal"
- Validation hook performs best for this pillar — naming the reality
  teachers live but can't say aloud
- Sources: use platform review data wherever possible. Query Neon for
  fresh data before producing Pillar 3 content.

**Data available from platform (Mar 2026 snapshot — verify against Neon DB
before using in any script; these figures change as reviews grow):**
- 34% of teachers say management quality is good
- 38% say they can give feedback to the principal
- 75% report salary is paid on time
- Average school rating: 3.26/5 (bimodal — schools cluster at high and
  low, few in the middle)
- 635 reviews, 555 schools, 133 districts

To get current figures: query Neon PostgreSQL (`stepper_form_data` table) or ask
Nikhil to provide a fresh CSV export before producing Pillar 3 content.

**Topic ideas:**
- "34% of teachers say management is good. What does good management
  actually look like?"
- Why teachers change schools every 1–2 years (data from staffroom)
- The salary-on-time problem: 1 in 4 teachers aren't paid on time
- What teachers wish they knew before joining their current school
- Red flags to watch for in a school interview

## Pillar 4: Teacher Wellbeing

**What it covers:** The emotional reality of teaching — burnout, recognition,
the idealism-reality gap, emotional spillover, what sustains teachers, and
the lack of support structures.

**Why it works:** "Teachers Are TIRED" — 35K views, **1,217 shares**. At
3.5% share rate this is the highest share-per-view in the channel. Teachers
shared it because it named their experience. Validation hook is the proven
format for this pillar. Dovetail confirms: recognition hunger, emotional
spillover, idealism-reality gap are live, unaddressed pain points across all
3 segments.

**Audience fit:** All segments but emotional texture differs. Segment 1: stress
of posting away from home, student disengagement. Segment 2: recognition
hunger, lack of formal feedback. Segment 3: emotional labour dismissed by
family, generational dismissal by older colleagues.

**Format principles:**
- Name the specific experience, not the abstract category. "You stayed
  back 2 hours for a student going through something hard. Your family
  asked why you couldn't just switch off." — not "teaching is stressful."
- Hinglish is especially effective for this pillar — emotional authenticity
  in native register.
- Do not amplify victimhood — acknowledge, then reframe toward dignity
  and resilience. staffroom is Tripadvisor, not a grievance platform.
- Children's love as the core counterweight: "main bacho ke pyar ke bigair
  nahi reh sakta" is an emotional truth that belongs in this pillar.

**Topic ideas:**
- "Teachers Are TIRED" (expand the franchise — what specific type of tired?)
- The moment teaching almost broke you — and what kept you going
- Why doesn't anyone talk about the emotional side of teaching?
- "I don't have a switch" — what emotional spillover really feels like
- The recognition teachers never get (but always need)
- Children's love: the thing no salary can replace

## Pillar 5: Platform Data Reveals

**What it covers:** Insights from staffroom's own teacher review database —
salary distributions, school rating patterns, management quality scores,
city-by-city data, subject-specific patterns. Content that only staffroom
can make.

**Why it works:** This pillar has not yet been fully exploited. It is the
strongest brand differentiator available — no other platform has this data,
and teachers will trust it because it comes from other teachers like them.
As review volume grows, this pillar's credibility compounds.

**Audience fit:** All segments. Strongest pull for career-decision-making
contexts (which school to join, which city to move to) — primarily Segments
2 and 3.

**Format principles:**
- Lead with the number, then explain what it means. "3.26 out of 5 — that
  is the average rating teachers give Indian private schools. Here's what
  that hides."
- Always cite the sample: "Based on X teacher experiences shared on
  staffroom across Y schools."
- Use bimodal/surprising patterns — the average is less interesting than
  the distribution. "Schools are not mediocre — they are either great or
  terrible."
- Keep live: query Neon before producing this content. Numbers change as
  reviews grow.

**Data to query before creating (Neon PostgreSQL):**
- Review ratings distribution (overall + per dimension)
- Salary medians by school type / city / subject
- Management quality scores
- School-switching frequency patterns
- City-by-city review volume and rating averages

**Topic ideas:**
- "We have 635 teacher experiences. Here's what they say about Indian
  private schools."
- The salary gap: what teachers earn in Bengaluru vs. the national average
- Why do schools either get great ratings or terrible ones? (bimodal data)
- Bengaluru schools: the best and worst-rated dimensions
- What 555 school reviews reveal about school management in India

## Pillar Balance

No single pillar should dominate a content calendar. Suggested monthly
distribution for a 12-piece calendar:

| Pillar | Pieces | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Career & Salary | 3 | Include at least 1 with specific salary numbers |
| Teaching Practice | 2–3 | At least 1 practical tool / technique |
| School Workplace Reality | 2–3 | Requires fresh platform data |
| Teacher Wellbeing | 2 | Use validation hook; no victimhood |
| Platform Data Reveals | 1–2 | Requires Neon query before writing |
