## Three User Segments

staffroom's target users are not a monolithic group. Research across 11 interviews surfaces three meaningfully different segments with distinct needs, emotional states, and digital behaviours.

### Segment 1: Government Low Income (Rural)
**Profiles:** Manisha (27F, Haryana, 3 months), Bhim Singh (36M, Haryana, 11 years), Anurag (34M, Haryana, 6 years), Nirpendra (34M, Madhya Pradesh, 9 years)

**Context:** Rural posting, long commutes, low student motivation, heavy admin burden. Several are unhappy with their current posting due to distance from home — this is a recurring stress driver, not a one-off complaint. Teacher shortage is not their immediate pain; student disengagement and institutional indifference are.

**Emotional state:** A mix of idealism and disillusionment. Nirpendra represents this most sharply — extensively trained in educational ideals, confronted daily by a system that "doesn't care if children actually learn, only if records show they passed." Children's affection ("main bacho ke pyar ke bigair nahi reh sakta") is the primary sustaining force.

**Peer support:** Bonding over shared struggles within school walls is their main support mechanism. For those living in teacher housing (Anurag lives with 7 others), peer learning happens informally and is highly valued. No formal mechanism exists for cross-school connection.

**Digital behaviour:** YouTube is the trusted resource — NCERT tutorials, subject explanations. Limited time for online content. WhatsApp groups exist but are admin-only. Not on social media platforms for professional use. Manisha: "does not have time to consume any online content."

**Content needs:** Classroom techniques that work with low-resource, low-motivation student contexts. Local context teaching examples. How to make classes engaging with minimal materials.

### Segment 2: Private Low Income (Urban)
**Profiles:** Swati (F, Karnataka, 8 years), Anees Fathima (38F, Karnataka, 7+ years), Anees + Amreen (paired, Karnataka, 9 and 8 years)

**Context:** Urban schools with resource constraints. Teacher shortage is real — Swati was given a Grade 3 Math class because the school had no dedicated teacher. Management support is inconsistent.

**Emotional state:** Career-oriented and subject-passionate. Recognition matters deeply. Anees Fathima actively seeks it — came second in a Kannada essay competition and mentions it unprompted as a high point. The Anees+Amreen paired interview showed how powerful explicit peer recognition is: both lit up hearing the other speak positively about them.

**Peer support:** Subject collaboration happens informally; formal structures (staff meetings) are admin-focused. Close friendships within school form around shared values, not just proximity.

**Digital behaviour:** More digitally active than Segment 1. Pinterest for worksheets (Swati), YouTube for lesson ideas. Swati doesn't use ChatGPT — "doesn't know how to use it" — but is keen to learn. Not part of substantive online teacher communities; most WhatsApp groups feel like spam.

**Content needs:** Practical teaching tools (worksheets, lesson plan templates, AI tools for teachers). Career information. Recognition and celebration of teaching craft.

### Segment 3: Private High Income (Urban)
**Profiles:** Annapoorna Nair (26F, Noida, 3 years), Pravin Rajguru (34M, Pune, 5+ years), Shivani (28F, Noida, 5 years, PhD student), Sugandhi Sharma (26F, Delhi, 3 years)

**Context:** Well-resourced schools with structured CPD, coordinator feedback, buddy systems. Better physical conditions but emotionally more complex. School culture increasingly feels like a "client relationship" with parents — Annapoorna: "mostly appeasing parents." Annapoorna also notes a generational divide: older teachers are dismissive of younger ones raising student welfare concerns.

**Emotional state:** High idealism confronting institutional compromise. Shivani's emotional spillover story is emblematic: a student in crisis kept her engaged well after school hours; her IT-professional husband told her to "switch off." The gendered dismissal of teaching's emotional demands is a live tension. Sugandhi's lowest point was resolved not by a solution but by the headmistress saying: "You know you are doing your best. Everyone here knows you are doing great." The need to be *seen* is acute.

**Peer support:** Official school channels (staybacks, CPD) are structured but often feel performative ("time waste"). Real support comes from informal friend groups, venting sessions after school, and bonding over shared frustrations — often about specific students or parents.

**Digital behaviour:** Personally active on Instagram but professionally disconnected from it. Shivani: "not a single teacher added to my profile." No online teaching communities. No creators followed (Sugandhi explicitly: "no online creators"). Research, when done, is informal — B.Ed colleague networks, YouTube, Google.

**Content needs:** Recognition of the emotional complexity of teaching. Stories of teaching victories (Sugandhi: "when it worked better than expected"). Career information, school culture comparisons. NOT generic pedagogy tips — "you learn more from classroom presence than CPD."

## Cross-Segment Insights

### Why teachers became teachers
No single answer, but recurring themes: (1) inspired by a specific teacher in their own life (Nirpendra, Anurag, Sugandhi); (2) perceived as a "good career for women" allowing flexibility (Swati); (3) noble profession / service orientation (Manisha, Anees Fathima); (4) fell in love with it after starting (Nirpendra: "when I started teaching, I fell in love with it"). Economic motivation with tuition side income appears in Segments 2 and 3 (Pravin).

### The recognition gap
Across all segments, recognition hunger is consistent and unmet. The specific form varies:
- Segment 1: Recognition from principals, peers, and the local community ("locals really respect teachers here" — Bhim Singh, noting this as a fading norm in broader society)
- Segment 2: Recognition from peers and school leadership for craft and knowledge
- Segment 3: Recognition from colleagues and school leadership for emotional labour

Actionable for staffroom: public recognition of teacher experiences (reviews, contributions) hits a deep, unmet need.

### Children's love as the core motivator
The single most consistent motivator across all 11 interviews is the relationship with students. Multiple verbatim quotes:
- "Main bacho ke pyar ke bigair nahi reh sakta" (Nirpendra)
- "Their love is most fulfilling, even if what they say is nonsensical most of the time" (Sugandhi)
- "I am very sociable with children" (Anees Fathima)
This is the emotional core of teaching identity. Content that speaks to this lands.

### The idealism-reality gap
Nirpendra articulates this most sharply, but the pattern appears across segments. Teachers enter with ideals (good pedagogy, student relationships, meaningful learning). They encounter: management that only cares about records, parents who treat school as a service, admin overload, posting they didn't choose. The gap between what they imagined and what they live is a source of low-grade, chronic disillusionment.
This is a live content opportunity — content that names this gap without exploiting it, and validates the frustration without amplifying victimhood, will resonate.

### Platform behaviour
- **YouTube** is the primary trusted content resource across all segments. Multiple unprompted mentions.
- **Instagram** is personal, not professional. Teachers do not follow teaching creators or join teaching communities on Instagram. They are reachable there for awareness content, but do not form community there.
- **WhatsApp groups** exist everywhere but are mostly admin-only or spam-heavy. Teachers have low tolerance for groups that don't deliver direct value.
- **No platform currently serves teachers as a professional community** — this gap is explicit in the interviews.

## Implications for staffroom

1. **The three segments have different content needs** — a single tone will not serve all. Segment 1 needs practical, grounded, low-resource classroom content. Segment 2 needs craft recognition and tools. Segment 3 needs emotional validation and systemic commentary.

2. **staffroom's review model directly addresses a real, articulated gap** — teachers want to know about school culture before joining. None of the existing platforms give them this. The D1 form completion data (30.7% completion rate — D1 counts all form-start attempts including abandoned starts before Section 0, giving a broader denominator than the ~40% figure in `staffroom-product-metrics.md` which measures completions among submitted reviews only) suggests the problem is not motivation — it's form friction.

3. **Recognition is the core emotional hook** — staffroom's ability to surface and celebrate teacher experiences is not a feature, it is the product's emotional reason for existing.

4. **YouTube is the channel to build on** — it is the only platform where teachers actively seek educational content and trust what they find.

5. **WhatsApp is functional, not community** — teachers will respond to direct value (useful information, nudges) but will not organically form community there.

6. **"Experience" as the platform action-verb creates a résumé mental model — confirmed**
   *Logged: 07-07-2026*

   Teachers structurally default to a job-application mental model when "experience" is used as the platform's action-verb near a school-search step. Two converging evidence sources:

   - **Confirmed incident:** A teacher wrote about her actual experience at School X but selected School Y (a school she wanted to work at) in the share-experience flow — apparently reading the prompt as "tell School Y about your experience" rather than "review this past employer." This is not inferred; it is a real, observed case.

   - **Code-level collision:** In `components/short-form/constants.ts` (product repo), the review form uses the word "experience" in two opposite senses within the same multi-step flow: `overallExperience` ("Rate your overall experience of working here" — retrospective, about this school) and `totalWorkExperience` ("How many years of total work experience do you have?" — résumé sense, career tenure). Both appear a few screens apart in the same form. The existence of a legitimately-named `totalWorkExperience` field proves "experience" cannot serve as the action-verb without ambiguity — the collision is structural, not incidental.

   **Decision taken:** staffroom is replacing "experience" with "review" as the core action word across all brand and content touchpoints (CTA: "Write a review" / "Write your review"). This is a brand-language correction, not a rebrand. The change to product code (`/share-experience` URL, form field copy) is a separate, already-handed-off engineering track.
